## Abstracts

June 3  

Sebastian Halbig  

From stabiliser codes to topological quantum computing  

One of the major challenges in quantum computing is error detection and error
correction. We begin with a review of quantum error-correction via stabiliser
codes using the language of group theory. As a particularly interesting
example, we examine Kitaev's toric code which is based on Z_2-symmetries.

The toric code, and the wider family of quantum double models, is a
paradigmatic example for topological quantum computing. Its favourable
error-correction properties arise from encoding information in the global
topological properties of the system, shielding the code space from "local"
errors. In the final part of the talk, we sketch a broad generalisation of
these quantum double models where groups are replaced by arbitrary Hopf
algebras (with invertible antipode). Here, the space of "logical qubits" arises
as a so called bitensor product—a representation theoretic construction that
combines coalgebra and algebra aspects. In the complex semisimple case the
relevant bitensor products coincide with invariant subspaces and we indicate
how an analogous statement in positive characteristic is related to Kaplansky's
5th conjecture.

May 13

Nadja Egner  

Galois theory and homology of quasi-abelian functor categories  

The notion of central extension in the sense of categorical Galois theory
unifies various classical notions of central extensions from algebra. Moreover,
it allows the development of a theory of higher central extensions and
generalized Hopf formulae for homology, which recover in particular the
Brown-Ellis-Hopf formulae for the integral homology of groups. In this talk, we
want to apply this general theory to study the category of T-shaped diagrams in
a quasi-abelian category A, where T is a finite category, i.e., the category
[T,A] of functors from T to A. More precisely, we will characterize the
(higher) central extensions and compute some generalized Hopf formulae in [T,A]
with respect to a full subcategory F of it induced by any full subcategory S of
T.

April 1

François Thilmany  

Center-preserving irreducible representations of finite groups   

A representation σ of a group G is called center-preserving on a subgroup H if
the only elements of H that become central under σ are those that were already
central in G. Through their favorable projective dynamics, this class of
representations came to light during recent work on the study of projective
representations of finite subgroups of invertible elements in integral group
rings.
After introducing this new yet elementary notion, we will sketch the proof of
the existence of such center-preserving representations under the condition
that the subgroup H admits a faithful irreducible representation.  Depending on
time, the connection with faithful (projective) representations and with free
products in group rings will be drawn.  The talk is based on joint work with
P.-E. Caprace and G. Janssens.

March 18  

Janis Gathot 

An introduction to quantum symmetry of graphs and a quantum version of Frucht's property  

We consider the notion of quantum symmetry for graphs - a non-commutative
generalisation of classical symmetry -, and discuss results on both quantum
automorphism groups and quantum isomorphisms of graphs. Also the notion of
graphs itself can be quantized. In this context we will discuss a quantum
version of Frucht's theorem, which in the classical case states that every
finite group appears as the automorphism group of some undirected connected
graph. If time allows it, we will also consider a characterization by Mančinska
and Roberson which states that determining quantum isomorphism of graphs is
equivalent to counting the number of homomorphisms from planar graphs.

March 4    

[Andrea Sciandra](https://www.andreasciandra.com/home)  

Quantum Yang–Baxter equation, Yetter–Drinfeld braces, and semi-abelian categories  

In this talk, we show how to construct solutions of the quantum Yang–Baxter
equation from Yetter–Drinfeld braces, algebraic structures that generalize
cocommutative Hopf braces and are equivalent to matched pairs of actions on
Hopf algebras. We further prove that cocommutative Yetter–Drinfeld braces
(equivalently, cocommutative Hopf braces) form a semi-abelian category that is
also strongly protomodular, as is the case for the categories of groups, skew
braces, and cocommutative Hopf algebras. Moreover, under suitable assumptions
on the base field, we show that skew braces and post-Lie algebras can be used
to construct a torsion theory for this category. This talk is partially based
on a joint work with D. Ferri and on a joint work with M. Gran.

February 25

Lukas Simons 

Different definitions of Nichols algebras  

Nichols algebras are certain braided Hopf algebras that play an
important role in the theory of Hopf algebras and quantum groups.
Originally introduced by Nichols, they have since revealed themselves
as rich objects with applications far beyond Hopf algebra theory. In
this talk, I will briefly outline the different definitions of Nichols
algebras and explain why these definitions are equivalent.

[Dimitri Leemans](https://leemans.dimitri.web.ulb.be)

Constructing coset geometries with Magma  

We will discuss briefly what is available currently in the Incidence Geometry
and Coset Geometry package to work with incidence geometries and subclasses of
them as abstract regular polytopes, abstract chiral polytopes, hypertopes, etc.
We will present some usual problems that one encounters when developing
algorithms in Magma.

**Francqui VUB-Leerstoel Inaugural Lecture**. On some beautiful children of abstract algebra  

From time to time, abstract algebraic structures like Nichols algebras produce
during their studies interesting, rather elementary but non-trivial
mathematical structures. In the lecture I will demonstrate two of such
structures, frieze patterns of integers and the hyperplane geometry of Weyl
groupoids.

February 11  

[Thomas Breuer](https://www.math.rwth-aachen.de/homes/Thomas.Breuer/)  

On the total degree of a finite group  

For a finite group $G$, the total degree $T(G)$ is defined as the sum of the
degrees of the complex irreducible characters of $G$.  We classify the groups
with $T(G) \leq 100$, using computational methods, and investigate special
cases such as groups of prime power order and groups with total degree the
square of a prime.  (This is joint work with László Héthelyi, Burkhard
Külshammer, and Magdolna Szöke.)  

The slides are [here](2026/breuer.pdf).
